Case Study

Guardian's Falls — Wounds

A 2.5D metroidvania built as a graduation thesis over 12 months with a team of 6. Started as an ambitious vision document with 8+ movement mechanics, 4 zones, and a deep narrative layer — shipped as a focused demo with a tighter core loop and hard-earned lessons in scope management.

Role Game Designer, Programmer & Producer
Team 6 people
Engine Unity 2021 · C#
Duration 12 months
Platform PC
Status Demo on itch.io
01 · Problem

Ambition outpaced capacity

The early design document — written in month 1 — envisioned 4+ zones, 8 parkour mechanics, a full combo-based combat system, skill trees, and layered narrative. The team had 6 people, no dedicated programmer, and 12 months.

02 · Process

Prototype fast, cut decisively

Built early prototypes with the full mechanic set. Tested which mechanics actually supported the core loop. Ran a structured beta test. Cut features that weren't pulling their weight — even ones the team loved.

03 · Solution

Ship what works, not everything

Delivered a focused demo across 3 zones with streamlined movement (sprint, slide, dash), dash-strike combat, environmental storytelling, and a linear progression that taught mechanics through level design.

04 · Outcome

Approved by committee

The examining committee acknowledged technical flaws and design questions but appreciated the full picture — the game, the process, and the team's ability to ship. Demo available on itch.io.

Scope Management

What We Envisioned vs. What Shipped

The original design document was written in months 1–2 of a 12-month timeline. It was ambitious by design — the goal was to dream big and then scope down to what the team could actually ship well. Here's what survived and why.

System Month 1 Vision What Shipped Status
Movement 8 mechanics: vault, slide, wall jump, grab/climb, swing, roll, sprint, free jump Sprint, slide, dash. Free jump and dashes (horizontal + vertical) added. Vault, wall jump, grab/climb, and swing prototyped but cut. Evolved
Combat 3-hit combo, slide attack, bounce attack, power punch (horizontal + vertical), sprint attack Streamlined to a dash-into-enemy-strike system. Combos and special attacks were in early prototypes but cut for clarity and feel. Simplified
Stamina / Resilience Stamina system, later planned as "resilience" (invulnerability mode with upgrades) Neither. The player can sprint and dash freely — removing the resource gate made movement feel faster and more fluid. Cut
Zones 4+ zones (Moonlit Meadow, Bond Fracture, Jade Jungle, Innerlake) + Hub 3 zones shipped: Moonlit Meadow, Jungle, and a Library area. Hub removed. No branching — linear progression. Simplified
Camera Dynamic switching between static and side-scroller per room type Side-scroller camera attached to the player throughout. Simpler, more predictable for level design. Simplified
Progression Skill tree, NPC quests, HUB, backtracking, left/right difficulty choice Linear progression through 3 zones. No skill tree or hub. Mechanical mastery is the progression. Cut
Narrative 4 cardinal guardians, trauma metaphor, intro cutscene, NPC dialogue, symbolic enemy design Environmental storytelling and interactions carry the narrative. No cutscene. Enemies are generic. The world-building comes through level design and environmental detail. Evolved
Enemies 3 types per zone + mini-boss + final boss, thematically symbolic designs Generic enemy types. The focus shifted to making combat feel responsive rather than narratively themed. Simplified
Environment Interaction Weapons on ground, barricading with doors/tables, destructible vine puzzles Basic environmental interactions shipped — enough to support the core loop and storytelling, not the full sandbox. Simplified
The pattern — Every cut either removed complexity that fought the core loop (stamina gate, branching paths) or reduced scope to something the team could polish in time (3 zones instead of 4+, linear instead of hub-based). The features that evolved — movement and narrative — changed shape but kept their purpose.
Design Process

Movement & Combat — From Doc to Demo

The original design doc specified exact acceleration values (80% max speed in 0.2s, 95% in 0.4s, 100% in 1s) and referenced specific games for each mechanic. That rigor mattered — but the real design happened in prototyping, when we discovered which mechanics felt good together and which ones fought each other.

Movement Feel

Ori and the Blind Forest

Fast acceleration curve, generous aerial control. Informed our sprint ramp-up values and how quickly the player should reach top speed.

Parkour Chains

Sifu · AC Chronicles: China

Chaining traversal moves (vault → slide → jump) creates a skill ceiling beyond platforming. Informed our slide+jump speed boost design.

Combat-Movement Sync

Hollow Knight · Megaman X

Attacks shouldn't break movement flow. Influenced the decision to make combat a dash-strike — momentum carries through the attack.

Core · Movement Shipped

Sprint + Slide + Dash

Design intent: Three movement layers that chain together. Sprint builds speed, slide maintains momentum while lowering the hitbox, and dashes (horizontal + vertical) extend reach and create aerial routes. No stamina cost — the player can chain freely.

Why it works: Removing the stamina gate from the original doc was one of the best cuts we made. Movement feels fast and expressive without a resource bar punishing experimentation.

Iteration: Early prototypes included vault, wall jump, and grab/climb alongside these. The team cut them — with that many inputs, movement felt chaotic instead of fluid. Reducing to 3 core mechanics made each one more readable and the levels easier to design around.

Core · Combat Evolved

Dash-Strike Combat

Design intent: Combat is movement. Instead of standing and executing combos, the player dashes into enemies to attack. This keeps the game's momentum consistent — you're always moving, even when fighting.

What changed: The original doc specified a 3-hit combo tree, slide attacks, bounce attacks, and two power punches (horizontal + vertical). Early prototypes had these working, but they pulled the feel in the opposite direction of the movement system. The combos made players stop and chain punches; the dash-strike kept them flowing.

Iteration: This wasn't in the original doc — it emerged from prototyping. Once we saw how the dash felt during traversal, applying that same momentum to combat was a natural evolution. The combo system was cut, but the core idea of "combat = movement" was stronger than the original plan.

Supporting · Narrative Simplified

Environmental Storytelling & Interactions

Design intent: The original vision included weapons on the ground, barricading with furniture, and destructible vine puzzles as narrative devices. The shipped version uses simpler environmental interactions to carry the story — the world tells the narrative that the missing cutscene would have.

What survived: Despite cutting the intro cutscene, thematic enemy designs, and NPC dialogue, the narrative still comes through strongly via environmental detail and interaction. The team's narrative designer ensured the world-building was embedded in the spaces themselves.

Quality Assurance

Beta Testing

Before the committee review, I designed and ran a structured beta test with categorized feedback: mechanics bugs, collision bugs, UI/menu bugs, difficulty assessment, control familiarity, and art/level/gameplay evaluation. Testers received a written guide and a Google Form for responses.

Testers

A few dozen

College peers, friends, and family. Not industry-qualified QA, but enough to surface the biggest issues — especially around level design and controls.

Biggest Finding

Level design pain points

Testers consistently struggled with level navigation. This was the most actionable feedback from the beta — and the hardest to fix under time pressure.

Control Iteration

Simplified inputs

Control familiarity was a known concern. We iterated by reducing the number of inputs, improving button mapping, and making the remaining controls more intuitive.

Process artifact: screenshot or embed of the beta testing guide (the structured categories document). Also: Google Form summary or a synthesized chart of the most common feedback themes.
Outcome

What Happened After

For the first 80% of the project, the team had a genuine goal of releasing on Steam. By the end, team morale had worn down — 12 months of production on a first project takes a toll. In the post-mortem, the team decided to leave Guardian's Falls as a demo. That decision was honest, and it was the right one.

The examining committee appreciated the full picture: the game itself, the production process, and the fact that we shipped something coherent despite the scope challenges. They noted technical flaws and had design questions, but their focus was on what we delivered — not what we cut.

Reflection

What I Carried Forward

Guardian's Falls was my first shipped team project. Everything I did differently on Idle Kingdom Builder and Idle Cyberpunk City Clicker started with lessons from here.

Scope is a design skill

The original doc had 8 movement mechanics. The shipped game has 3 — and they feel better than 8 ever would have. Cutting isn't failure; it's focus. This mindset defined how I built both idle games: start with the minimum viable loop, not the maximum vision.

Test before you're ready

The beta test wasn't transformative, but it caught level design issues and control pain points we'd become blind to. Even imperfect testing with non-expert players surfaces problems you can't see from the inside.

Mechanics must talk to each other

The combo combat system fought the movement system — they wanted different things from the player. The dash-strike emerged because we prototyped both and noticed the conflict. Design documents are hypotheses; prototypes are experiments.
