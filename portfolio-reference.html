Case Study — Idle Cyberpunk City Clicker

220 tracked players · pre-1.40 build · 4 milestone snapshots
01 · Problem

Flying blind after the first game

Idle Kingdom Builder shipped with 800+ downloads and solid reviews, but no analytics pipeline — I had no idea where players dropped off or why. Cyberpunk City had 8,700+ downloads and the same blind spot. I needed a system to see inside the player journey without breaking the budget.

02 · Process

Milestone-based analytics on a budget

Needed maximum data with minimum event sends (free Unity Analytics tier). Designed 4 snapshot events at 1K, 1M, 1B, and 1T credits — capturing buildings, upgrades, click/passive ratio, and time elapsed per player.

03 · Findings

Economy validates, bugs surface

Click-to-passive transition confirmed as designed. Found a tracking bug inflating clicker counts (variable naming mistake) and a timer persistence defect at higher milestones producing invalid data.

04 · Outcome

3 fixes shipped for v1.40

Corrected analytics instrumentation, adjusted passive upgrade multiplier from 5× to 3.5×, and added session persistence. Clean data incoming for before/after comparison in the next analysis cycle.

Case Study — Guardian's Falls

Team of 6 · Scope management · Graduation project
01 · Problem

Ambitious scope vs. reality

The original design was far larger than what a team of 6 could ship under academic deadlines. Art and sound deliverables created pipeline bottlenecks that blocked programming work.

02 · Process

Role shifted to fill gaps

Started as game designer, but pivoted to developer/producer as the project needed someone building systems and unblocking the pipeline. Designed systems I couldn't test until assets arrived, built modular to adapt.

03 · Solution

Scope cuts to ship

Identified what was feasible to cut without breaking the core experience. Presented options to the team and made collective decisions on scope. The final product was a focused vertical slice of the original vision.

04 · Outcome

Shipped a playable demo

Delivered a working 2.5D Metroidvania demo with parkour mechanics. Well received by the examining committee and new audiences. Those who knew the original scope were less impressed — a lesson in expectation management.

Process artifacts: original vs. final scope comparison, production timeline, pipeline bottleneck analysis, parkour mechanic iteration, team coordination docs.
Production story — This isn't framed as a design showcase — it's a production and scope management story. "Led scope cuts to ship a playable vertical slice with 6 people under academic deadlines" is more valuable to a hiring manager than pretending everything went smoothly.

About

Photo

Game designer with a statistics background and a build-first, iterate-always approach. I bring a structured, evidence-based methodology to whatever design problem I'm facing — whether that's economy tuning, mechanics, levels, or narrative. 5+ years shipping 12+ titles across mobile, PC, and web. Currently based in São Carlos, Brazil.

Design philosophy I design systems players feel they discovered on their own — then use data to make sure they actually can. When players feel lost, that's not a badge of honor, it's data showing where the invisible guidance failed. Every design is a hypothesis. Build, ship, measure, improve.

That approach comes from studying how Valve teaches Portal's mechanics through play and guides Half-Life players with lighting and architecture instead of waypoints — invisible design validated by obsessive playtesting. Kojima's influence shows up in the ambition: trusting players with complex systems and meaningful choices, not dumbing things down, but making depth accessible through careful iteration.

Outside of shipping games, I teach game development and design to kids and teens — 200+ students across three schools. It's taught me to break down complex systems into clear, communicable concepts, which directly improves how I write documentation and present design intent to teams.

I'm also drawn to narrative and quest design — building worlds players want to inhabit and writing quests that feel like natural parts of that world. It shows up in the Genshin Impact quest doc I wrote, in Bagre Noturno's alternate-reality story, and in the atmospheric lore behind Cyberpunk City. It's a growing area for me, not a primary specialty yet, but one I actively pursue.
